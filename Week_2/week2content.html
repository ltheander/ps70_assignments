<br> ### Week 2: 2D Design and Cutting ![image of one press-fit sculpture](./Sculp2pic.jpg) <br> <h3>Press-Fit Construction</h3> <p>Having never used a modeling program like Fusion 360, I had to start with learning the basics. Looking back now, I wish that I had done the tutorial section of the assignment before trying to model shapes to use in press-fit construction. Instead, I learned how to use Fusion 360 through trial and error (with a lot of error). I now know that I used many inefficient methods and what should have been a five minute job to simply model the outline of the shape I wanted turned out to be much longer. While it was not the most efficient way to go about learning Fusion 360, it did teach me the best ways not to do things in Fusion so that I can be more efficient in the future.</p> <p>In creating my shape for my press-fit construction, I wanted to do something similar to the design of the nano bots from Big Hero 6. These bots, roughly cylindrical shaped with sloped edges leading to thinner ends, could combine in any direction to create any desired structure. To do something similar, I made my shapes with slightly sharper edges for ease of combination. I knew I wanted my shapes to be able to combine in multiple dimensions, so I added attachment points on the long edges and at the ends. I originally had a small circular hole in the center of the design, but replaced this with a rectangular hole that can fit the long end snugly to enable building in three dimensions off of a single piece.</p> ![image of my shape in fusion](./Shape1pic.png) <p>While cutting a 18 by 32 inch piece of cardboard to fit into the laser cutter, I measured the thickness of the cardboard and found it to be 4.25 mm.</p> ![image of cutting the cardboard](./Cut1pic.jpg) <p>At first, I overestimated the amount of kerf that would occur. On my first test, I made sure my connectors were 4.05 mm wide but after laser cutting, I found that the combination of predicting too much kerf and having the settings on the laser cutter cutting too hot caused the connectors to be slightly too loose.After the laser cutter settings became more standardized as various classmates and TF's learned what worked and what didn't, I adjusted my model to have connector widths of 4.10 mm and extended the depth of the lengthwise connections to allow for a greater surface area in the connection and create a tighter connection. This ended up being a perfect width for my press-fit construction, and I ended up printing around 20 of my shapes to be able to assemble in various arrangements.</p> ![image of laser cutter cutting my project](./Cut2pic.jpg) <p>On top is the first version of the printed shape and the bottom is the final version</p> ![image of first and second versions of my shape](./Cut3pic.jpg) <p>I built three different structures using my press-fit construction shapes. The first was a sort of hybrid between a plane and a scorpion.</p> ![image of first sculpture](./Sculp1pic.jpg) <p>The second took more inspiration from trees as I tested how high I could construct while keeping a stable base. Asymetrical root-like structures ended up being the best supporting base for this taller structure.</p> ![image of second sculpture](./Sculp3pic.jpg) <p>The third structure was more dynamic. I made a flick-football game in which half of a shape which was part of the first loose-fitting series is flicked through the structure to score points.</p> ![image of third sculpture](./Sculp5pic.jpg) ![image of third sculpture](./Sculp6pic.jpg) <p>If I were to do this project again, there are several things I would do differently. I would have started my learning of Fusion 360 with a few in-depth tutorials rather than try and figure it out on my own like I ended up doing. I also would have chosen a shape that was slightly wider. Because my shape was so narrow, only one connection on the elongate sides could be used at once, and shapes that had attachments on both an elongate side and the center connector did not fit well due to competition for the same binding site.</p> <br> <h3>Fusion 360 Tutorial Build</h3> <p>After creating my press-fit kit, I used an online tutorial to create a 3D model of a lego brick. During the tutorial, I learned many tricks and tips that I wish I had known before starting to design my press-fit construction shape. The tutorial started with extruding a rectangle to create a box, on which I added a circle that was extruded to create a cylinder.</p> ![image of modeling the lego](./Block1pic.png) <p>With the tutorial, I then used the rectangular pattern tool to multiply the cylinders to get eight that were spaced correctly.</p> ![image of modeling the lego](./Block2pic.png) <p>Next, I shelled the bottom of the block to hollow it out.</p> ![image of modeling the lego](./Block3pic.png) <p>Then, I created three hollow cylinders inside the hollowed out block by creating circles, extruding them, using the shell tool, and using the rectangular pattern tool.</p> ![image of modeling the lego](./Block4pic.png) <p>Finally, I used the fillet tool to smooth the top edges of the lego brick and was left with this completed model.</p> ![image of modeling the lego](./Block5pic.png) <br> <h3>Modeling Household Objects</h3> <p>The two household objects that I decided to model in Fusion 360 were a rubiks cube and a wall charger.</p> ![image of rubiks cube](./Rubiks0pic.jpg) ![image of wall charger](./Plug0pic.jpg) <p>I first used the caliper to measure all of the important distances on both objects and jotted down the measurements so that I could work on the modeling at home.</p> ![image of measurements](./Sketch1pic.jpg) <p>I started with the rubiks cube and tried several different ways of construction mostly centering on building a single cube and multiplying it using rectangular construction to get the final shape.</p> ![image of first cube](./Rubiks1pic.png) ![image of multiplied cube](./Rubiks2pic.png) ![image of full cube](./Rubiks4pic.png) <p>Once I had the final shape, I used the fillet tool to add the finer details of the cube. I found it very difficult to select the correct interior edges to fillet once I had multiplied the shape. Because of this, I experimented with several other method of construction such as creating filleted cubes and combining them in the right combination to get the final shape. However, because I wanted a cube that was completely connected and didn't want to add detail to each individual section of the cube, I stuck with the original multiplicative design.</p> ![image of detailed cube](./Rubiks3pic.png) <p>I then made my wall charger. This process was much simpler, both because the filleting was much less complex and because I had already learned some extra tricks while making the rubkis cube. I first made a square, rounded off the corners with the fillet tool, and extruded to make the main body of the charger.</p> ![image of plug body](./Plug1pic.png) <p>Next, I added the prongs and used the circle and hole tools to make the holes in them perfectly lined up. </p> ![image of plug body with prongs](./Plug2pic.png) <p>I finished off the charger by adding in a port at the bottom by extruding a shape into the main body. I want to learn how to do this using the shell tool instead as I haven't gotten a chance to use it much yet but I will be watching more tutorials to increase my Fusion 360 knowledge.</p> ![image of plug body with port](./Plug3pic.png) <p>We were then tasked with using the two objects to make an assembly. Because my objects didn't go very well with each other, I turned my rubiks cube into a wall charger holder by setting it on top.</p> ![image of plug and cube](./Joint1pic.png) ![image of plug above cube](./Joint2pic.png) ![image of plug on cube](./Joint3pic.png) And here is a [link to where the cover image came from](https://www.deviantart.com/midnightfreakshow/art/2D-Design-Project-1-Pattern-Bat-401932787/).
